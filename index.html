Analysis of Claim Filings & Causes

Understanding why growers file claims is crucial. While adverse weather (freeze, drought, heat) remains the primary driver, disease represents a significant and highly complex segment of the claims landscape for clonal crops due to their perennial nature and high establishment costs.

The Impact of Plant Disease

Unlike annual row crops, clonal crops (vines, bushes, trees) are heavily impacted by systemic diseases. Growers purchase insurance specifically to protect against catastrophic yield failure due to unpreventable pathogen outbreaks.

  • 🍇
    Grapes: Pierce's Disease, Powdery Mildew, and Botrytis Bunch Rot. Wildfire smoke taint (though technically environmental) often triggers similar quality-loss claims.
  • 🌰
    Nut Trees: Walnut Blight, Phytophthora Root Rot, and Almond Hull Rot. Often exacerbated by unseasonal rains.
  • 🍓
    Berries: Anthracnose, Verticillium Wilt, and Mummy Berry. Highly sensitive to localized humidity and soil conditions.

Policy Changes & Settlement Dynamics

Over the past 25 years, the Risk Management Agency (RMA) and major private insurers (AIPs) have evolved their products. The focus has shifted from simple yield protection to revenue protection and specific endorsements for quality loss.

1. The "Good Farming Practices" (GFP) Mandate

Historically, disease claims were frequently denied. Modern settlements strictly require documentation of GFP. If a nursery or grower applied standard preventative fungicides and followed extension protocols, but a pathogen (like Pierce's Disease) still caused catastrophic loss due to abnormal weather patterns, the claim is paid. Failure to document GFP results in zero indemnity.

2. Rise of Whole-Farm Revenue Protection (WFRP)

Introduced to accommodate highly diversified specialty crop operations (common in berries and grapes). Instead of insuring individual blocks of strawberries against specific perils, WFRP insures the farm's historical gross revenue. This heavily mitigates complex, multi-peril disease outbreaks that degrade quality but don't entirely destroy the yield.

3. Quality Loss Enhancements

Crucial for grapes (wine). Smoke taint from wildfires or specific rots may not reduce the tonnage of grapes harvested, but drops the market value to zero. Policy changes around 2018-2020 explicitly expanded "quality loss" adjustments, allowing growers to claim settlements based on the rejection of the crop by buyers, not just physical destruction.

4. Settlement Calculations

Typically, growers purchase coverage levels between 50% and 75% of their historical yield/revenue. When a disease outbreak occurs, the settlement is calculated based on the production to count against the guarantee. High-value clonal crops often utilize "Actual Production History" (APH) plans.
